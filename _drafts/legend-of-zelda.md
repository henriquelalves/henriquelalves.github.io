---
title: Zelda (NES) multiplayer experience
lead: This is a brief analysis for an over-analyzed game - a quick peak on the things that made the last temple of Zelda a remarkable experience, coming from someone that finished it just recently for the first time.
---

First, a disclaimer: I didn't like the first Legend of Zelda. Mind you, doesn't mind I *didn't enjoy it* - but that's kinda the same blurry line that separates laughing *with* you or *at* you. I recently played it out of nostalgia value, along with an adventurous friend that agreed on tackling the game head-on, without walkthroughs.

And let me tell you, it was a nightmare.

The game has the kind of unfair try-hard, arbitrariness difficulty that made me feel exploration as a mindless trial and error testing *ad naueseaum*. I would most certainly drop the game if I wasn't playing it "multiplayer-like" - me and my friend would always be changing controllers when someone died, and in the overworld, the one not playing would be drawing a map (which became our triumph token, a 100% completed map with most secrets in-game discovered). That multiplayer experience was the enjoyable thing about Zelda, talking our way out of dungeons by thinking together which wall looked "explosible", or finding together the temples and secrets.

I would digress more about my personal experience, but I'll leave it to another moment. I know most of my displeasure comes from the "modern gamer" mind, so I'll set myself in saying that the first Zelda had some undeniable try-hard exploration moments; even so, I completely understand why this game is GOOD in it's on way, and how it made so much success - it is a solid experience, and this culminates on the last Zelda temple. 

By *solid experience*, I mean that even the game try-hard exploration is consistent; it isn't a thrown out experience, but one of the core gameplay elements. The game would reward you for trying everything everywhere, for finding the secret old-mans, for fighting every enemy in all dungeon rooms.
